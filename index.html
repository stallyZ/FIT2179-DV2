Free Fares, Full Platforms?

Victoria made trains, trams and buses free from 31 March to 31 May 2026. Instead of claiming results that are not yet fully available in open data, this visualisation looks at the recent baseline: which services were already carrying the most demand before the fare-free period?

This project uses recent Victorian Government public transport data. The 2026 free-fare period is used as background, but I do not use it as a final measured result because the open data is not fully available yet. I mainly want to show which services and stations were already busy, so viewers can understand where extra passengers may be noticed first. Readable Vega-Lite chart descriptions are available in specs/charts.json.

The fare change is recent; the pressure was already uneven.

Free travel is simple as a policy message, but demand is not evenly spread across modes or days. These charts use the latest open patronage records as a baseline. This is helpful because the free-fare period is quite recent, so I should not guess its final impact without enough data.

1. Recent daily patronage by mode

Metro trains and trams dominate the passenger baseline, while regional services operate at a smaller scale but may still feel large changes locally.

Source: Monthly average patronage by day type and by mode, Victorian Government Data Directory.

2. 2025 demand by mode and day type

Normal weekdays carry the strongest routine demand, while weekends and school-holiday weekdays reveal more discretionary travel patterns.

5. Weekend compared with normal weekdays

This ratio helps separate commuter-heavy services from modes where weekend demand is closer to weekday demand.

A smaller weekend gap suggests demand may be more leisure or event sensitive.

The map points to concentrated rail demand.

Station-level entries show where the rail network already concentrates passengers. Free fares may be statewide, but the practical experience of crowding is likely to be felt first at major interchange and CBD stations.

6. Metropolitan train stations by annual entries

The map shows that rail demand is not spread evenly across Melbourne; a small number of central and interchange stations carry much larger volumes.

Source: Annual metropolitan train station patronage, FY2024-25. Circle size uses annual station entries.

7. Busiest train stations

The busiest stations are mostly CBD or interchange locations, which makes them important pressure points during fare changes.

Top 20 stations by annual entries.

8. Peak-period shape at major stations

Different stations have different daily rhythms, so extra demand would not necessarily arrive at the same time everywhere.

Top 12 stations, entries by time band.

9. Weekday versus weekend station use

Most stations with high weekday use also have higher Saturday use, but the relationship is uneven across the network.

Each dot is a metropolitan train station.

Weekend intensity highlights busy stations where non-commute travel already matters, a useful clue for fare-free travel effects.
